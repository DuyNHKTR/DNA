# Document summary v2 for `document.pdf`

## 1. Bibliographic information

- **Title:** DNA Sequence Classification Using Machine Learning Models Based on k-mer Features
- **Author:** Afthar Kautsar
- **Journal:** Journal of Computers and Digital Business (JCBD)
- **Issue:** Volume 4, Number 2, May 2025, pages 100-105
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.56427/jcbd.v4i2.762

## 2. Research objective

The paper studies whether machine learning models can classify **cell-free DNA (cfDNA)** fragments into normal and mutation/tumor-related classes using **3-mer frequency features**. The practical motivation is that cfDNA can be used as a non-invasive biomarker for cancer detection and monitoring.

## 3. Method and dataset

### Dataset described in the methodology

- DNA length: **126,033 bp**
- Fragmentation with **k = 3**
- Total fragments: **630**
- Train/test split:
  - **504** training samples
  - **126** testing samples

### Feature representation

Each DNA fragment is converted into a numeric vector based on **3-mer frequency counts**.

### Models evaluated

- **Random Forest**
- **Support Vector Machine (SVM)**
- **Deep Neural Network (DNN)**

### Evaluation metrics

- Accuracy
- Precision
- Recall
- F1-score

## 4. Main results

### Overall accuracy

- **Random Forest:** 71%
- **SVM:** 70%
- **DNN:** 67%

Based on the tables and conclusion, **Random Forest is the best-performing model in this experiment**.

### Class-wise behavior

All models classify the **mutation** class much better than the **normal** class.

#### Random Forest

- `precision(normal) = 0.75`
- `recall(normal) = 0.08`
- `precision(mutation) = 0.71`
- `recall(mutation) = 0.99`

#### SVM

- `precision(normal) = 0.00`
- `recall(normal) = 0.00`
- `precision(mutation) = 0.70`
- `recall(mutation) = 1.00`

#### DNN

- `precision(normal) = 0.38`
- `recall(normal) = 0.13`
- `precision(mutation) = 0.71`
- `recall(mutation) = 0.91`

## 5. Interpretation

The headline result is not really that the models are strong; it is that they are **biased toward predicting mutation**.

The paper itself points to two likely causes:

- **Class imbalance**
- **Simple k-mer representation**

From the reported numbers, that reading is reasonable. The models can identify mutation-related patterns, but they generalize poorly to the normal class. In particular, SVM completely fails on normal samples, and Random Forest reaches the best overall accuracy largely because the mutation class dominates performance.

## 6. Limitations visible in the paper

This paper has several methodological or reporting weaknesses:

- The models perform poorly on the normal class, so overall accuracy alone is misleading.
- The paper does not clearly document model settings or hyperparameters.
- The origin and labeling process of the data are not explained in enough detail for reproducibility.
- The feature engineering is basic and no stronger baselines are tested.
- The discussion does not quantify whether the class imbalance was handled during training.

## 7. Internal inconsistencies in the paper

This is the most important caution when using this paper.

### Inconsistency A: best model

- The **abstract** says **DNN achieved the highest classification performance**
- The **results tables** and **conclusion** say **Random Forest achieved the highest accuracy (71%)**

### Inconsistency B: model list

- The **abstract** mentions **Decision Tree (DT), SVM, and DNN**
- The **methodology and results** report **Random Forest, SVM, and DNN**

This suggests the abstract may not match the final experiment description.

### Inconsistency C: dataset size

- The **abstract** mentions **3,000 DNA sequences**
- The **methodology** describes **630 DNA fragments** derived from a DNA sequence of **126,033 bp**

These are materially different dataset descriptions and should be treated as a reporting problem.

## 8. Practical takeaway

The paper is useful as a **small, introductory comparison** showing that k-mer frequency features can support cfDNA classification. However, it is **not a strong or clean experimental reference** because:

- class performance is highly imbalanced
- reporting is inconsistent
- the setup is under-specified

If this paper is cited, it should be cited cautiously and preferably only for the general idea that k-mer-based features can be used with ML models for DNA/cfDNA classification.

This paper reports that Random Forest, SVM, and DNN can classify cfDNA fragments using 3-mer features, with Random Forest reaching the highest reported accuracy at 71%. But the experiment is strongly skewed toward the mutation class, and the paper contains multiple internal inconsistencies. The result is useful as a rough exploratory study, not as a high-confidence benchmark.
